THE PHYSICAL TRANSFORMER

Section 1 of 5 — Architecture Overview
1. Architecture 2. Neural Network 3. Mechanisms 4. Signal Flow 5. Dimensions

The Physical Transformer is a kinetic sculpture that physically computes a neural network. Not a simulation. Not a metaphor. The machine actually learns — it trains on data encoded in a pin drum, adjusts its own weights through self-locking worm gears, and converges to correct predictions over epochs of mesmerizing mechanical motion.

After training, a viewer turns three input dials, and the machine thinks — levers pivot, brass rods transmit signals through a transparent midplane, and a three-sided answer prism clicks into position with the prediction.

DESIGN BREAKTHROUGHS
Breakthrough #1 — The Bidirectional Lever

The same weighted Archimedes lever computes both the forward pass AND backpropagation. Drive inputs from one end = inference. Drive error from the other end = learning. This eliminates ball-and-disk integrators entirely and makes the machine's deepest truth visible: backprop IS the forward pass in reverse.

Breakthrough #2 — The Escapement Heartbeat

A clock escapement replaces the governor. The machine doesn't compute continuously — it computes in discrete beats, like a thinking brain. Each tick evaluates one neuron. The rhythm is audible: tick... tick... tick. You can hear the machine thinking.

Breakthrough #3 — Brass Rods, Not Strings

At 300mm scale, strings either droop limply or need impractical tension. Thin rigid brass rods (1.5mm) through a transparent acrylic midplane transmit signals instantly with zero compliance. The golden lines cutting through clear acrylic are more beautiful than any string.

Breakthrough #4 — Single Lever vs Compound Pantograph

A 9-stage compound pantograph loses 37-77% of its signal to friction (0.95^9). One weighted Archimedes lever with 9 sliding pivots does the same computation with 2 friction points instead of 18. Simpler, more reliable, more Margolin.

Breakthrough #5 — The Whippletree as Calder Mobile

Loss computation uses cascaded balance beams — a whippletree. When the network is wrong, the beams tilt dramatically. As it learns, they settle toward level. Add an eddy-current damper and the settling motion becomes a slow, graceful dance. Like a Calder mobile finding equilibrium.
