# Vashisht Collisions — Mechanism Cross-Pollination

## Philosophy
Tiers are dead. "Planetary is Tier 1" is engineering thinking, not art thinking.
What happens when you take the SOUL of one mechanism and JAM it into the BODY of another?
What happens when a 12th-century Indian mercury wheel meets a 1940s Curta calculator?
What happens when you stop asking "what's most efficient?" and start asking "what's never existed?"

This document is the collision lab. Every entry takes TWO or more ideas from completely different worlds and breeds a mutant.

## COLLISION 1: Tide Predictor + Cam-on-Cam + Karakuri Puppet Theater
### "The Living Tide"

**Parents:**
- Lord Kelvin's Tide Predictor (1872) — single continuous thread through pulleys summing harmonics
- Cam-on-Cam stacking — one cam lifts the base of another
- Karakuri Ningyo — wooden automata with whale baleen springs, tea-serving puppets

**The Mutant:**
A SINGLE continuous cable winds through the ENTIRE sculpture — entering at one edge, snaking through every node, exiting at the other edge. At each node, the cable passes over a small CAM STACK (two stacked cams on perpendicular shafts from the X and Y axes). The cam stack physically displaces the cable's path up or down. The pixel hangs from the cable at that point.

But here's the Karakuri twist: the cams are WOODEN, hand-carved, mounted on wooden shafts in wooden frames. The entire overhead mechanism looks like the inside of a Japanese mechanical puppet — warm, organic, visible wood grain. The cable is thick braided cotton cord, not invisible fishing line.

**What it creates:** A single unbroken line of motion flowing through the entire sculpture. Tug the cable at one end and the wave propagates physically. The wooden cam mechanism creaks and ticks — the SOUND is part of the art. It's a tide predictor you can hear predicting.

**Why it hasn't been done:** Kelvin's predictor used metal pulleys and thin wire. Margolin uses invisible string. Nobody has made the cable THE star — thick, visible, physically continuous — with hand-carved wooden cams at every node.
