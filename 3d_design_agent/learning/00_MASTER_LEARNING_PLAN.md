# PART 2E: HOW TO PRACTICE (Science-Backed)

🧠 **LEARN THIS** — HOW you practice determines growth rate. No AI can practice for you.

### Deliberate Practice (Ericsson)
Each session targets a specific sub-skill slightly beyond current ability. Don't just "build stuff" — identify what's weakest (cam profiles? assembly? tolerance management?) and drill that specifically. Ask yourself: "What am I bad at?" Then practice THAT.

### Interleaving (Not Blocking)
Within each week, alternate between mechanism types. Don't spend a whole week on cams then a whole week on linkages.

**Example week:**
- Monday: Crank-slider (30 min cardboard) + Fusion Level 2 (1 hr)
- Wednesday: Four-bar linkage (30 min) + Fusion Level 3 (1 hr)
- Friday: Return to crank-slider variant + Cam intro (cardboard)
- Weekend: Integration (combine two mechanisms) + Daughter automata

> **This feels slower but produces 20-40% better long-term retention** (Shea & Morgan 1979, replicated across dozens of motor learning studies). Blocked practice feels productive but leads to overestimation of learning.

### Spaced Repetition for Physical Skills
Build the same mechanism type every 2-3 weeks with progressive variations:
- Week 1: Basic cam follower
- Week 3: Asymmetric cam (quick rise, slow fall)
- Week 6: Multi-lobe cam (2 actions per rotation)
- Week 10: Cam with dwell period
- Week 14: Return to basic cam — notice how much faster you are

### Flow State Calibration
Rate each project's challenge (1-10) and your current skill (1-10). Aim for **challenge = skill + 1 or +2**.

| You Feel | Diagnosis | Fix |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| Bored, going through motions | Challenge too low | Add constraints (smaller scale, tighter tolerances, new material, time limit) |
| Frustrated, wanting to quit | Challenge too high | Simplify, return to recently mastered mechanism, break into sub-skills |
| Absorbed, time disappears | **Flow state** — optimal | Keep going. Document what you did so you can recreate this calibration |

### Retrieval Practice
Before starting any build:
1. Sketch the mechanism from memory FIRST (no references)
2. Try to design parameter set from scratch BEFORE consulting recipes
3. After each build, write what you learned WITHOUT looking at notes

This forces active recall — the most powerful learning technique known.

### The "20-Hour First Competence" Rule (Josh Kaufman)
Each new mechanism type needs approximately **20 hours** to reach basic competence. Don't abandon a mechanism after 3 hours of frustration. Expect the first 5 hours to feel terrible. That's normal. Competence arrives around hour 15-20.

# PART 2F: THE EMOTIONAL JOURNEY OF LEARNING

🧠 **LEARN THIS** — Managing frustration and plateaus is THE skill that separates people who quit from people who succeed.

### Expert Wisdom on Starting Badly

> **Tim Hunkin** (Cambridge engineer, *Secret Life of Machines*):
> "I told them they just had to make things badly to start with and gradually they'll get better. **I made things badly for the first half of my life.**"

> **Cabaret Mechanical Theatre** (the world's leading automata workshop):
> "Making automata is hard. It requires a wide range of construction skills and understanding of mechanical and engineering principles. **This can be very frustrating to the beginner, but help is available.**"

> **Arthur Ganson** (MIT Museum, 30+ years of kinetic machines):
> "When I started, I was making things far more complex than necessary. **Now I'm getting more simple, bringing things down to the essence.** Every part is there for a reason."

### The "Ugly First Prototype" Psychology
Creating and criticizing activate **conflicting brain regions**. Trying to do both simultaneously causes your mind to fight itself. This is why perfectionism kills creative output.

**The rule:** Build the full mechanism through to completion, EVEN IF it's terrible. No editing during creation. Evaluate AFTER you've finished. Then iterate.

### Growth Mindset (Carol Dweck)
**"Not yet" framing:** Your first cam will be ugly. Your first four-bar will bind. Your first assembly will have interference.

This is not failure — it's data. Every kinetic sculptor in history went through this phase. The difference between people who quit and people who succeed is ONE WORD: **yet**.

- ~~"I can't build a four-bar linkage"~~ → "I can't build a four-bar linkage **yet**"
- ~~"My cam profile doesn't work"~~ → "My cam profile doesn't work **yet**"

### Plateau Map: What to Expect

| When | What Happens | Why | Fix |
|------|-------------|-----|-----|
| Month 2-3 | Mechanisms feel repetitive | Initial novelty worn off, real skill acquisition hasn't clicked yet | Interleave mechanism types, try a new material, attend a workshop |
| Month 6-7 | Integration harder than expected | Combining mechanisms reveals gaps in individual skills | Drill the weakest sub-skill, seek feedback from community |
| Month 10-12 | Professional quality seems far away | Taste outpaces ability (the "taste gap") | Study one expert deeply, rebuild one of their designs, compare |
| Month 15+ | "Am I really getting better?" | Diminishing returns at higher levels | Tackle something you've been avoiding, teach someone, seek critique |

### Jansen's "Extinction" Mindset
Theo Jansen doesn't call his failed Strandbeests "failures." They "go extinct." He keeps a boneyard.

**Your boneyard is your learning record.** Save every failed prototype. Write one sentence on why it failed. In 6 months, you'll have a library of lessons no textbook contains.

## Session Structure (For Variable Schedule)

**Principle:** Every session is SELF-CONTAINED. No "continue where you left off" — you might have 2 days between sessions.

### Weekday Session (2-3 hrs)
```
SESSION START (10 min): Review last session's photos/notes. Set ONE goal.
MAIN WORK (90-120 min): ONE mechanism build, test, or Fusion session.
SESSION END (15 min): Photo result. Write 3 sentences. Note next session goal.
```

**Good goals:** "Build cardboard [mechanism]" • "Model [X] in Fusion with joints" • "Print and test [component]"
**Bad goals:** "Work on sculpture" (too vague) • "Learn about mechanisms" (not actionable)

MAIN WORK (4 hrs): Major build, integration, or iteration cycle.
DOCUMENT (30 min): Full documentation, photo/video, pattern capture.
