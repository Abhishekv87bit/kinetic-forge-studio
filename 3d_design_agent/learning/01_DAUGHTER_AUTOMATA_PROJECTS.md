# Daughter Automata Projects
## Building Together: You Cut/Print, She Colors/Plays

**Design Principle:** Start at age 4 with NO-mechanism toys, progress to mechanisms by age 5-6.
**Session Length:** 15-30 minutes (age 4-5), 30-60 minutes (age 6+)
**Total Projects:** 15+ over 18 months

📖 **Key Reference:** Robert Addams, "Automata Design", p.64-69 - "AUTOMATA FOR YOUNGER PEOPLE"

# PROJECT 0A: Spinning Pinwheel
**Age 4 | Mechanism: Simple Rotation (No Linkage!)**

## What It Does
A colorful pinwheel spins when she blows on it.

## What She Learns
- Air makes things move
- Round things spin
- Colors blend when spinning fast

## Materials
- Square paper or card (15cm × 15cm)
- Pencil or stick
- Pin or tack
- Markers/crayons

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the paper with bright colors
- [ ] Choose the colors
- [ ] Blow to make it spin
- [ ] Try different blowing angles

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut the diagonal slits
- [ ] Fold and pin to stick
- [ ] Ensure it spins freely

## Session Plan
**Single session (15 min):** Color, assemble, play!

## Teaching Moment
"When you blow, the air pushes the paper! The harder you blow, the faster it spins!"

# PROJECT 0B: Lever See-Saw
**Age 4 | Mechanism: First-Class Lever**

📖 **See:** Robert Addams p.45 for lever diagram

## What It Does
Two animals go up and down on a see-saw when you press one end.

## What She Learns
- Push down here, that goes up
- Balance point is in the middle
- Levers are everywhere!

## Materials
- Cardboard strip (~20cm × 4cm)
- Cardboard triangle (fulcrum)
- Two small toy figures or cardboard animals
- Markers

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the see-saw board
- [ ] Color/choose the animals
- [ ] Put animals on ends
- [ ] Make them go up and down!

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut see-saw and fulcrum
- [ ] Balance the fulcrum position
- [ ] Make stable base

## Mechanism Diagram
```
    ANIMAL          ANIMAL
       ↓               ↓
   ────●───────────────●────
              △
           FULCRUM
```

## Session Plan
**Single session (20 min):** Color animals, build see-saw, play!

## Teaching Moment
"This is called a LEVER. Watch - when I push down here, the other side goes UP! You try!"

# PROJECT 0C: Nodding Bird
**Age 4-5 | Mechanism: Weighted Lever (Gravity Toy)**

📖 **See:** Robert Addams p.65 for detailed diagram

## What It Does
A bird on a perch nods its head up and down when you tip the base.

## What She Learns
- Heavy things fall down
- Balance can create motion
- Gravity is a force

## Materials
- Cardboard for bird and base
- Small weight (coin, washer)
- Pivot point (brass fastener)
- Markers

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the bird
- [ ] Add eyes and beak details
- [ ] Add feather decorations
- [ ] Tip the base to make bird nod

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut bird shape
- [ ] Attach weight to bird's beak
- [ ] Create pivot point
- [ ] Make stable base

## Mechanism Diagram
```
         ◠◡◠  ← Head with weight
           \
            ●── pivot
           /
     [  BASE  ]
     tip here →
```

## Session Plan
**Single session (20 min):** Color bird, you assemble, she plays!

## Teaching Moment
"The bird has a heavy bead! When I tip this way, the heavy part falls down and the head goes UP. Then it swings back!"

# PROJECT 0D: Pecking Chicken ⭐ CLASSIC
**Age 4-5 | Mechanism: Gravity + Paddle Weight (NO Mechanism!)**

📖 **See:** Robert Addams p.65 - This is the PERFECT first "automaton"

## What It Does
Chickens on a board peck when you swing a pendulum weight underneath.

## What She Learns
- Swinging weight creates movement
- Multiple things can move at once
- This is how old toys worked!

## Materials
- Cardboard for chickens and base
- String
- Weight (small ball of clay, button)
- Beads for chicken heads
- Markers, craft feathers

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the chickens
- [ ] Add craft feather tails
- [ ] Add googly eyes
- [ ] Swing the paddle, watch them peck!

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut chicken shapes
- [ ] Create the paddle mechanism
- [ ] Thread strings through board
- [ ] Attach weight

## Mechanism Diagram
```
     🐔    🐔    🐔    🐔  ← chickens on strings
      │     │     │     │
   ═══●═════●═════●═════●═══  ← board with holes
              │
              │ ← string
              ●
           WEIGHT
         (swings)
```

## Session Plan
**Session 1 (20 min):** Color chickens, add feathers
**Session 2 (20 min):** Assembly, play!

## Teaching Moment
"Watch the ball swing back and forth... and the chickens PECK! The swinging pulls the strings, and the strings pull the chicken heads down. When the string relaxes, the heads pop back up!"

# PROJECT 0E: Jumping Jack
**Age 4-5 | Mechanism: String Pull Linkage**

📖 **See:** Robert Addams p.66 - threading diagram included

## What It Does
A figure's arms and legs fly up when you pull a string.

## What She Learns
- Pulling one string moves FOUR limbs
- Things can be connected invisibly
- Puppet mechanics!

## Materials
- Cardboard for body, arms, legs, head
- Brass fasteners (5)
- String
- Markers, decorations

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the figure (person, animal, robot?)
- [ ] Add face details
- [ ] Decorate clothes/costume
- [ ] Pull string to make it jump!

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut body parts
- [ ] Punch holes for fasteners
- [ ] Thread string correctly (see book diagram!)
- [ ] Assemble with fasteners loose enough to move

## Mechanism Diagram
```
         ◯  ← head
         │
    ─────●─────  ← arms pivot
         │
         │
    ─────●─────  ← legs pivot
         │
         ●═══════► PULL!
       string
```

## Session Plan
**Session 1 (20 min):** Color all parts
**Session 2 (30 min):** Assembly (tricky threading!), play!

## Teaching Moment
"See how the string goes behind the arms and legs? When you pull, it pulls ALL of them at once! That's why they all jump together!"

# PROJECT 1: Dancing Sun
**Phase 1 | Mechanism: Simple Crank**

## What It Does
A sun with a smiling face bobs up and down when you turn the handle.

## What She Learns
- Turning a handle makes something move
- The crank turns round and round, the sun goes up and down

## Materials
- Corrugated cardboard (cereal box works)
- Brass paper fasteners (3)
- Wooden skewer or dowel for handle
- Crayons/markers for decorating

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the sun face (before assembly)
- [ ] Add sun rays with yellow/orange markers
- [ ] Turn the handle to make sun dance
- [ ] Name the sun!

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut sun circle (~8cm diameter)
- [ ] Cut crank disk (~5cm diameter)
- [ ] Cut connecting rod
- [ ] Punch holes for fasteners
- [ ] Assemble mechanism
- [ ] Add handle

## Mechanism Diagram
```
      SUN
       O
       |  ← Connecting rod
       |
   ────●──── ← Crank (rotating)
       |
     HANDLE
```

## Session Plan
**Session 1 (30 min):** Color the sun, you cut parts
**Session 2 (30 min):** Assembly together, play!

## Teaching Moment
"Watch! When I turn the handle around and around, the sun goes up... and down... up... and down! The stick in the middle changes spinning into bobbing."

# PROJECT 5: Hopping Bunny
**Phase 2 | Mechanism: Cam Follower**

## What It Does
A bunny hops up and down with a smooth bounce motion.

## What She Learns
- Bumpy circles make things jump
- The shape of the bump controls the hop

## Materials
- Cardboard
- Brass fasteners
- Cotton balls for tail
- Googly eyes
- Egg-shaped cam (you cut)

## Her Role
- [ ] Color the bunny
- [ ] Add cotton ball tail
- [ ] Add googly eyes
- [ ] Add whiskers (pipe cleaners or drawn)
- [ ] Turn handle to make bunny hop
- [ ] Count the hops!

## Your Role
- [ ] Cut bunny shape
- [ ] Cut egg-shaped cam (this is the key!)
- [ ] Create guide for vertical motion
- [ ] Assemble so bunny rides on cam
- [ ] Ensure smooth hopping motion

## Mechanism Diagram
```
         BUNNY
           │
           │  ← Follower (rides on cam)
           ▼
       ╭─────╮
      ╱       ╲ ← Egg-shaped cam
     │    ●    │  (offset center)
      ╲       ╱
       ╰─────╯
           │
        HANDLE
```

## Cam Shapes to Try
- Egg = Gentle hop, pause at bottom
- Heart = Double hop per rotation
- Snail = Slow rise, quick drop

## Session Plan
**Session 1 (30 min):** Color bunny, add cotton tail
**Session 2 (45 min):** Assembly, test hopping
**Session 3 (optional):** Try different cam shapes!

## Teaching Moment
"See how the wheel isn't a perfect circle? It has a bump! When the bump comes around, the bunny goes UP. When the flat part comes around, bunny goes DOWN. The shape controls the hop!"

- Gather materials
- Clear workspace
- Put on "project clothes" (messy OK!)

1. **Show & Tell** (5 min): Show her the finished mechanism, let her explore
2. **Her Part** (15-30 min): Coloring, decorating, adding materials
3. **Your Part** (parallel): Cutting, assembling mechanism
4. **Together** (5-10 min): Final assembly, testing

- Clean up together
- Display the project

# Tips for Success

## Her Attention Span
- 4-year-old: 15-20 minutes focused
- 6-year-old: 30-40 minutes focused
- 8-year-old: 45-60 minutes focused
- **Split into multiple sessions!**

## When She Loses Interest
- Take a break
- Let her play with finished projects
- Come back tomorrow
- It's supposed to be fun!

## When Things Don't Work
- "Hmm, let's figure this out together!"
- Show her it's OK to try again
- "Engineers always have to fix things"
- She learns problem-solving by watching you

## Safety Notes
- You handle scissors, hot glue, sharp tools
- She handles coloring, decorating, turning handles
- Brass fasteners have sharp backs - bend them flat
- Supervise small parts (choking hazard at 4)

# GRANDMASTER PROTOCOL: 2-Year Intensive Automata Lab

## The Philosophy: Focused Learning Through Play

**Duration:** 2 years intensive (age 4 to 6), then ongoing casual play
**Goal:** 25-30 automata in 2 years = mechanism mastery through repetition

Your daughter is 4. By the time she's 6, you'll have built 25-30 automata together. That's enough reps to make mechanisms second nature. After that, you keep playing -- but the intensive "learning through teaching" phase is complete.

**Why 2 years is enough:**
- You already understand mechanisms (Lego Technic)
- 25-30 builds = every mechanism type multiple times
- By year 2, you're not learning -- you're creating
- The play continues forever, but focused training has a finish line

---

## The 6 Automata Mechanisms (Mapped to Kinetic Sculpture Equivalents)

From your automata book, these are the building blocks. Each maps to a kinetic sculpture mechanism:

| Automata Mechanism | Kinetic Equivalent | Motion Type | Daughter-Friendly Name |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Cam & Follower** | Cam & Follower | Up-down bobbing | "The Bouncer" |
| **Crank Slider** | Slider-Crank | Up-down pumping | "The Nodder" |
| **Eccentric Disc** | Eccentric Drive | Wobbly rotation | "The Wiggler" |
| **Four-Bar Linkage** | Four-Bar Linkage | Side-to-side wave | "The Waver" |
| **Ratchet & Pawl** | Geneva (intermittent) | Click-click stepping | "The Clicker" |
| **Friction Drive** | Belt/Gear Drive | Spin transfer | "The Spinner" |

## 2-Year Intensive Project Progression

## The Automata Session Template (Weekend Play)

A 5-step format for each weekend build session:

```
1. THE STORY (10 min)
   "What should our toy do today?"
   Let her describe the motion she wants
   You recognize: "That sounds like a CAM!"

2. THE BUILD (30-60 min)
   Cardboard prototype first (always)
   She decorates while you cut
   Assemble together

3. THE TEST (10 min)
   "Turn the handle!"
   What works? What doesn't?
   SHE diagnoses: "It's stuck!" YOU identify why

4. THE FIX (10-20 min)
   Iterate together
   This is where YOU learn the most

5. THE NAME (5 min)
   She names the creation
   Photo for the collection
   Add to "Our Automata Museum" shelf
```
